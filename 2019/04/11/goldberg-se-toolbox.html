Book notes - Systems Engineering Toolbox for Design Oriented Engineers

Chapter 1: Introduction

Definitions

  • A tool is defined as a set of procedures to accomplish a specific function.
  • A methodology is defined as a collection of tools, rules, and postulates to accomplish a purpose.

Types of tools

Tool Description
Concept development tools ... are useful when selecting the preferred option of several alternatives. Among these alternatives are such things as cost, complexity, weight, safety, manufacturability, or perhaps determining the ratio of expected future benefits to the expected future costs.
System safety and reliability tools ... address the following areas of concern: (1) identify and assess hazards, (2) identify failure modes and show their consequences or effects, and (3) symbolic logic modeling tools used to understand the failure mechanisms of the system. These tools are also used to determine the probability of failure occurring or the reliability that a component will operate successfully, either in comparative or absolute terms, as applicable.
Design-related analytical tools ... are applied to show (1) which parameters affect a system the most or least, (2) a method for specifying dimensions and tolerances, and (3) the determination of the possibility or probability of having form, fit, or function problems with a design, or to determine a tolerance or dimension necessary to avoid these problems.
Graphical data interpretation tools ... are applied when there is a desire to monitor performance, identify relationships, or reveal the most important variables in a set of data.
Statistical tools and methodologies ... compare sample statistics and population statistics. Variations are identified and mathematical relationships are determined.
Total quality management (TQM) tools ... are applied to continuously improve performance at all levels of operation, in all areas of an organization, using all available human and capital resources.
Quantitative tools ... that are used to identify potentially hazardous conditions based on past empirical data are trend analysis tools. The ultimate objective for these tools is to assess the current status, and to forecast future events.

Chapter 2: Concept Development Tools

Tool Description
Trade Studies Trade studies are used to quantitatively or qualitatively compare alternatives to select the most preferred one. They are categorized as either weighted factor trade studies, or its special version, the analytical hierarchy trade study. The former is performed when the options under consideration as well as program requirements are well defined, while the latter is used when comparing alternatives pairwise and when it is easier to generate preferences using subjective verbal expressions rather than numerical values.
Advantages: to be realized are (i) resources expended can be commensurate with the benefits of the task, (ii) technique can be adapted to prioritization based upon programmatic and technical considerations, (iii) identification of disadvantages of an option can lead to effective countermeasures, and (iv) the method provides a clearly documented analysis in which objectives and requirements, alternatives, and methodology are recorded.
Limitations: include (i) the technique depends on the analyst's expertise and amount of available data, (ii) the assessment can be prejudiced and result in incorrect results if inappropriate selection criteria, weights, and utility functions are used, (iii) the number of alternatives considered are limited by amount of analysis resources, (iv) the options to be evaluated must be determined prior to analysis, and (v) the weighting factors and advantages/disadvantages are subjective.
Cost-Versus-Benefit Studies Trade studies are used to quantitatively or qualitatively compare alternatives to select the most preferred one. They are categorized as either weighted factor trade studies, or its special version, the analytical hierarchy trade study. The former is performed when the options under consideration as well as program requirements are well defined, while the latter is used when comparing alternatives pairwise and when it is easier to generate preferences using subjective verbal expressions rather than numerical values. These are typically done early in the project, though they can be done whenever a method is needed to select alternatives. A trade study analysis allows a systematic approach to evaluation of design options and may also help delineate the most important system requirements.
Advantages: to be realized are (i) the analyst can assess the cost effectiveness of several alternatives over the entire life cycle, and (ii) the method provides a clearly documented analysis in which objectives and requirements, alternatives, and methodology are recorded.
Limitations: include (i) the analysis is flawed if system requirements are incomplete or inadequate - the costs can be underestimated if the operating environment is not well understood or characterized and benefits cannot be well addressed if requirements are too vague or general, (ii) an incomplete list of alternatives leads to incomplete analysis, (iii) the analysis is flawed if incomplete or inaccurate cost estimates are used, (iv) the benefits must be quantified even if they are intangible, insubstantial, or difficult to state in monetary terms, and (v) the method does not take into account technical factors besides cost, so this tool is generally insufficient for selection of large, new design efforts, but more appropriate to production-level design solutions..
